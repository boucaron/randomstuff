# Why Open Source Needs a New Business Model

Open source software (OSS) powers almost every part of our digital world — from government systems and public services to companies and personal apps. Yet the current model is broken: everyone benefits, but very few give back, leaving maintainers overworked and projects underfunded. It’s time for a **fairer, sustainable approach** that accounts for both maintenance and innovation.

**Open Source in a Nutshell:**

* OSS is software whose **source code is freely available** for anyone to use, modify, and share.
* It drives innovation, collaboration, and accessibility worldwide.
* Despite its ubiquity, **most projects are at risk** because funding, maintenance, and governance are scarce.

**Why We Need a New Business Model:**

1. **Sustainability & Maintenance** – Maintainers need reliable funding to cover ongoing work: feature development, bug fixes, documentation, security patches, and infrastructure upkeep.
2. **Fairness for All Users** – Everyone benefiting from OSS — companies, governments, institutions, NGOs — should contribute their fair share.
3. **Feature-Driven Innovation** – Users and organizations can fund the features they actually need, ensuring development aligns with real priorities.
4. **Balanced Governance & Backpressure** – Structured influence prevents disruptive changes while allowing maintainers to innovate responsibly.
5. **Transparency & Trust** – Clear legal and fiscal structures ensure funds are managed effectively and maintainers are protected.
6. **Community Protection** – Free access is preserved for individuals, small projects, and non-profits.
7. **Aligned Incentives & Support for Small Innovators** – Those who benefit most contribute most, while small contributors and projects still have mechanisms to sustain and innovate.

**In short:** OSS built the digital backbone we all rely on, but the current model leaves maintainers to shoulder the cost alone. A new business model ensures **fair funding, responsible governance, and long-term innovation**, keeping OSS strong for everyone — companies, states, institutions, and individuals alike.

## Why, How, and What is a License?

A software license is **the legal text that defines what you can and cannot do with software**. In OSS, licenses **shape who benefits, who contributes, and how projects survive**.

### Main License Types

| License Type                           | Key Features                                                              | Impact                                  | Drawback                                                       |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Permissive (MIT, BSD, Apache)**      | Use, modify, redistribute freely, even in proprietary products            | Maximum adoption                        | No requirement to contribute back; free-rider problem persists |
| **Copyleft (GPLv2, GPLv3)**            | Must share modifications if redistributed                                 | Protects software freedom               | Strong copyleft may discourage some commercial use             |
| **Weak Copyleft (LGPLv2, LGPLv3)**     | Allows linking with proprietary software                                  | Compromise between freedom and adoption | Partial protection                                             |
| **Commercial-Aware (AGPL, SSPL, BSL)** | Introduce payment or contribution requirements for commercial/network use | Forces commercial users to contribute   | Not OSI-approved; seen as “non-free” by purists                |

**Why this matters:** Licenses **directly determine who pays, who contributes, and who has influence**. Modern licenses allow OSS to **enforce minimum contributions**, giving maintainers legal and economic tools to survive in a world where everyone — companies, governments, institutions — depends on their work.

## License and the Business Models

Licenses are **the legal lever** that enables OSS business models. Combining a thoughtful license with a strategic funding approach ensures projects are **functional, secure, evolving, and accessible**.

| Business Model                             | License Fit                         | Funding Mechanism                           | Example                                   |
| ------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Dual Licensing                             | Permissive or copyleft + commercial | Paid commercial license                     | MySQL                                     |
| Support / Consulting                       | Any OSS license                     | Expertise, installation, training           | Red Hat Enterprise Linux                  |
| Cloud / SaaS                               | Commercial-aware or permissive      | Subscription fees for hosted service        | MongoDB Atlas, GitLab.com                 |
| Feature-Based Crowdfunding                 | Any OSS license                     | Users fund specific features/milestones     | Tailwind / OpenCollective projects        |
| Enterprise Subscriptions / Paid Editions   | Commercial-aware                    | Paid enhanced features                      | GitLab EE, ElasticSearch commercial tiers |
| Minimum Contribution / Commercial-Use Fees | SSPL, BSL                           | Baseline contribution from commercial users | SSPL MongoDB                              |
| Patronage / Sponsorship                    | Any OSS license                     | Recurring donations, memberships            | GitHub Sponsors, Mozilla Foundation       |
| Hybrid Models                              | Combinations                        | Multiple revenue streams                    | MongoDB: SSPL + Atlas + support           |

**Takeaway:** By strategically pairing license type and business model, maintainers can:

* Keep software **free for individuals and small users**
* Ensure **commercial users contribute fairly**
* Fund both **maintenance and feature development**
* Maintain **governance balance and stability**
* Build a **resilient, sustainable ecosystem**

## Limitations of Existing Business Models

Current OSS models **fail to fully capture value and ensure sustainability**, leaving critical projects vulnerable:

1. **Fragile Support Models** – Many projects rely on selling consulting or training, but **most users do not pay**, and AI/LLMs reduce demand for traditional support.
2. **Cloud / SaaS Capture Most Profit** – Large providers host OSS, capture value, and rarely contribute back.
3. **Forking Risks** – Companies may fork projects for their own services, fragmenting ecosystems (e.g., Redis, Elasticsearch).
4. **Insufficient Patronage** – Donations and memberships rarely cover maintenance for large, widely used software.
5. **Free-Rider Problem** – Companies, governments, and institutions use OSS extensively **without contributing**, socializing costs onto a few volunteers.
6. **AI and Automation Pressure** – Modern AI accelerates adoption but reduces revenue from support or consulting.

**Maintenance and innovation costs** are often invisible, yet critical — without proper funding, **security, stability, and progress slow**, threatening the ecosystem.

## Reduced Innovations

OSS is everywhere yet undervalued. Many projects rely on small teams juggling **feature work, bug fixes, security, and infrastructure maintenance**. Some must **paywall features**, others fall behind.

Most people think OSS is **“free like free beer”**, forgetting the real costs: **time, expertise, infrastructure, and care**. True OSS freedom is about **choice and responsibility**, not zero cost.

OSS is not the next sexy startup — it’s **quiet infrastructure**, invisible but essential. Without stable funding, **innovation slows and maintenance suffers**. We cannot **build on moving sand**: breaking APIs arbitrarily destroys trust, stability, and the ability to innovate.

**Linus Torvalds’ principle:** *never break user-space*: Once users rely on a behavior — even a bug — it becomes part of the de facto API. Arbitrarily breaking it destroys ecosystem trust.

Structured **funding, governance, and backpressure** allow OSS to **survive, maintain, and innovate responsibly**, ensuring software is stable, predictable, and robust.

## Core Principles for a New Business Model of OSS

1. **Mandatory Minimum Contributions for Commercial & Institutional Users** – Covers maintenance, feature development, and reduces free-rider effects.
2. **Feature-Driven Funding (Crowdfunding / Milestones)** – Prioritize and fund development aligned with real-world needs.
3. **Governance & Backpressure** – Users and contributors influence large projects; preserves API stability and trust.
4. **Transparency & Fiscal Structure** – Open management of funds via **foundations, nonprofits, or associations** builds trust and accountability.
5. **Preserving Freedom for Individuals & Small Projects** – Ensures grassroots innovation, learning, and experimentation remain viable.
6. **Alignment of Incentives** – Those who benefit most contribute most; financial incentives are proportional to usage and benefit.
7. **Support for Small Contributors & Innovation at All Scales** – Mechanisms to sustain small teams, solo developers, or niche projects foster experimentation and the next wave of innovation.

By embedding these principles in **licenses, funding, and governance**, OSS can transition from **fragile, volunteer-dependent projects** to **resilient, thriving ecosystems**, ensuring both **maintenance and innovation are sustainable** over the long term.
